SUPPORT PLAYBOOK — CUSTOMER SUCCESS TEAM
==========================================

RULE 1: Customer escalates directly to their CSM about a support ticket that has been open for more than 7 days
ACTION: Acknowledge the frustration immediately and take personal ownership of the issue. Pull up the ticket history and identify where the delay occurred. Contact the Support team lead directly to get a status update and a firm resolution timeline within 4 hours. Communicate that timeline back to the customer the same day with your name as the single point of contact until it is resolved. Add a recurring internal reminder to follow up every 24 hours until closure.
REASON: When a customer bypasses support and reaches out to their CSM, they have already lost confidence in the normal process. Taking ownership and providing a specific timeline restores trust. Leaving them in the regular queue after they have escalated signals that no one is listening.

RULE 2: Customer sends a frustrated email or message saying support quality has declined overall
ACTION: Do not respond defensively or redirect them back to support. Schedule a call within 48 hours to listen and document their specific experiences. Before the call, review their last 90 days of support interactions including ticket volume, average resolution time, CSAT scores, and any repeat issues. On the call, present what you found, acknowledge gaps honestly, and propose a concrete improvement plan such as a dedicated support contact, priority routing, or weekly status syncs for open issues.
REASON: General complaints about support quality are rarely about a single incident. They reflect accumulated frustration over multiple interactions. Responding with data shows you take their experience seriously, and proposing structural changes demonstrates that you are solving the pattern, not just apologizing for the latest symptom.

RULE 3: Customer has multiple open tickets across different product areas at the same time
ACTION: Consolidate visibility by creating an internal tracker for the account that lists every open ticket, its owner, its priority, and its expected resolution date. Share a summarized version with the customer so they can see that everything is being tracked in one place. Coordinate with Support and Engineering leads to ensure tickets are not being worked in isolation and that dependencies between issues are identified. Provide the customer with a single weekly status update covering all items.
REASON: When a customer has several open issues, each ticket may be progressing independently but the customer experiences it as chaos. Nobody on their side should have to chase five different threads. A consolidated view reduces their effort, prevents duplicate work internally, and shows organizational competence during a vulnerable moment.

RULE 4: Customer threatens to churn or post a negative review because of a bad support experience
ACTION: Treat this as a critical account escalation. Notify your manager and Account Executive within one hour. Reach out to the customer personally and set up a call within 24 hours with a senior leader from your team, ideally a Director or VP. On the call, listen fully before responding, acknowledge the failure without excuses, and present an immediate remediation plan. Follow up in writing with a summary of commitments and deadlines. Track completion of every item and report back to the customer upon resolution.
REASON: Threats to churn or go public are last-resort signals. The customer has likely been frustrated for a while and feels unheard. Escalating internally and involving senior leadership shows the complaint is being taken at the highest level. The goal is not just to fix the issue but to demonstrate that the company's response to failure is fundamentally different from the experience that caused the complaint.

RULE 5: Customer reports the same issue returning after it was marked as resolved
ACTION: Reopen the original ticket and escalate it to the Engineering team as a regression or incomplete fix. Do not route it through the standard triage queue. Inform the customer that you are treating this as a priority re-escalation, not a new ticket, and provide an updated timeline. Internally, request a root cause analysis from Engineering to understand why the fix did not hold. Share the findings with the customer once available, along with the steps being taken to prevent recurrence.
REASON: Recurring issues destroy credibility faster than new ones. The customer trusted that the problem was solved, and reopening it feels like a betrayal of that trust. Treating it as a regression rather than a fresh ticket signals that you recognize the failure and are holding your own team accountable for a durable fix.

RULE 6: Customer's support satisfaction scores have dropped below acceptable thresholds over the last quarter
ACTION: Proactively schedule a relationship review meeting before the customer raises it themselves. Prepare a candid assessment of their support experience including average first response time, resolution time trends, ticket reopen rates, and CSAT scores compared to their previous quarter and your company benchmarks. Present the data transparently, own any shortfalls, and propose targeted improvements such as adjusting their support tier, assigning a technical account manager, or implementing proactive monitoring for their most common issue categories. Set a 30-day checkpoint to measure whether the changes are working.
REASON: Waiting for a customer to complain about declining support quality means you have already lost ground. Approaching them first with the data and a plan positions you as a proactive partner, not a reactive vendor. It also prevents the dissatisfaction from compounding into a renewal risk or an executive escalation that is harder to recover from.
